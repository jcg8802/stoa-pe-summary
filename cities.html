Top 10 City Deep Dives
Rank #1 · North Carolina
Asheville, NC
89
Asheville sits in the Southern Appalachian corridor — ground zero for the fentanyl epidemic in rural NC. OD death rate is among NC's highest (~48/100K), yet licensed SUD residential beds are almost nonexistent (0.4/100K vs. statewide avg 2.1). NC's November 2023 Medicaid expansion added 600K newly eligible adults; Asheville-area enrollees are dramatically underserved. No CON law means fast market entry. Mountain tourism economy creates a secondary private-pay opportunity.
Ideal Facility Profile
50–70 beds · ASAM 3.5 + 3.7 detox · Co-occurring MH/SUD · MAT-integrated
Key Referral Sources
Mission Health (HCA), VA Asheville, Buncombe County Drug Court, Pisgah Legal Services, jail diversion programs
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$4.2–5.1M/yr at 85% occupancy · 70% Medicaid · 30% commercial
Key Risks
Rural staffing (nursing shortage), higher real estate than eastern NC, mountain geography limits patient transport radius
Rank #2 · North Carolina
Charlotte, NC
87
Charlotte is the fastest-growing major city in the Southeast (2.7M MSA, adding ~100K/yr). Medicaid expansion in 2023 created a wave of newly eligible SUD patients in Mecklenburg County. Existing Medicaid residential SUD beds are severely limited — most Charlotte-area residential facilities operate private pay or out-of-network. The 2023 expansion demand has not been met by supply. Strong commercial employer base (Bank of America, Truist, Duke Energy HQ) creates secondary commercial payer opportunity.
Ideal Facility Profile
80–100 beds · ASAM 3.5 + 3.7 + IOP/PHP · Dual-payer (Medicaid + commercial) · Acquire existing license
Key Referral Sources
Atrium Health (Carolinas Medical), Novant Health, Mecklenburg County Drug Court, CLT Rescue Mission, law enforcement diversion
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$6.5–8.0M/yr at 88% occupancy · 65% Medicaid · 35% commercial (employer base)
Key Risks
Higher real estate costs vs. smaller markets, competitive landscape for staff, CON-free means other entrants moving fast post-expansion
Rank #3 · Arkansas
Little Rock, AR
86
Little Rock is the single best geographic adjacency play for Stoa. Arkansas borders Louisiana, shares similar regulatory culture and demographics, has had full Medicaid expansion since 2014 (Arkansas Works), and an active IMD waiver. AR has only 1.8 SUD facilities per 100K — the statewide treatment gap is severe. Central Little Rock MSA is the hub for the entire state's referral network. Stoa's existing clinical playbook, billing protocols, and MCO relationships transfer almost unchanged.
Ideal Facility Profile
50–70 beds · ASAM 3.5 + 3.7 · MAT program · De novo or small acquisition
Key Referral Sources
UAMS Medical Center, AR Children's Hospital network, Pulaski County Drug Court, AR Dept of Corrections reentry, CABHA network
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$3.8–4.6M/yr at 85% occupancy · 80% Medicaid · 20% commercial
Key Risks
Smaller market than NC cities; AR Medicaid rates slightly below LA (~$285 vs $335/day); limited behavioral health workforce pipeline
Rank #4 · North Carolina
Greensboro / Winston-Salem, NC
82
The Triad (Greensboro + Winston-Salem + High Point) is a 1.7M MSA that flies under the radar relative to Charlotte and Raleigh. Post-2023 Medicaid expansion demand is concentrated here among the working-class and low-income population. OD rates are above NC average. Real estate costs are 30–40% lower than Charlotte, improving unit economics. The Triad has strong hospital infrastructure (Cone Health, Novant, Wake Forest Baptist) as referral anchors. Competition is lighter than Charlotte.
Ideal Facility Profile
60–80 beds · ASAM 3.5 + 3.7 + PHP · Dual-diagnosis track · Lower real estate = higher EBITDA margin
Key Referral Sources
Cone Health, Wake Forest Baptist Medical Center, Forsyth County Drug Court, Guilford Co. jail diversion, NC DPS reentry
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$4.8–6.0M/yr at 87% occupancy · 72% Medicaid · 28% commercial
Key Risks
More economically challenged workforce market than Charlotte/Raleigh; reimbursement same as Charlotte but lower commercial ceiling
Rank #5 · West Virginia
Huntington, WV
81
Cabell County (Huntington) holds the highest opioid overdose death rate of any county in the United States — approximately 90 per 100K, nearly 6x the national average. WV has full Medicaid expansion and an active IMD waiver. The demand is extreme and unambiguous. Despite being a small MSA (350K), the per-capita need justifies a specialized facility. Multiple academic studies (Marshall University, Johns Hopkins) have been conducted here — the clinical research credibility boosts grant funding eligibility. SAMHSA consistently designates this area for priority funding.
Ideal Facility Profile
40–60 beds · Heavy 3.7 detox capacity · MAT/buprenorphine program · Leverage SAMHSA grant funding
Key Referral Sources
Cabell Huntington Hospital, Marshall Health, WV Drug Courts, local harm reduction programs (SOAR), law enforcement/fire dept MAT programs
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$3.0–3.8M/yr at 88% occupancy · 85% Medicaid · Heavy detox revenue
Key Risks
Very rural, small population limits census ceiling; staffing is chronically difficult (WV workforce crisis); existing providers well-established; geographic distance from Stoa LA base
Rank #6 · Arkansas
NW Arkansas (Fayetteville/Springdale/Rogers)
80
NW Arkansas is the fastest-growing metropolitan area in the entire Southeast, adding over 20,000 residents per year. Walmart HQ, Tyson Foods, J.B. Hunt — Fortune 500 employers create a genuine commercial insurance population alongside significant blue-collar Medicaid-eligible workers. SUD treatment infrastructure has not kept pace with population growth. The meth/opioid problem is severe in Benton and Washington Counties. AR Medicaid expanded. No CON law. The commercial payer opportunity here is the highest of any AR city.
Ideal Facility Profile
60–80 beds · ASAM 3.5 + PHP/IOP · Target dual payer — lean into commercial given employer base · Springdale or Rogers submarket
Key Referral Sources
Washington Regional Medical, Mercy Hospital NW AR, Benton County Drug Court, AR DHS DCFS network, Walmart EAP
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$4.5–5.8M/yr at 86% · 60% Medicaid · 40% commercial (best commercial ratio in AR)
Key Risks
Real estate costs rising fast (Bentonville effect); staffing competition from Walmart/Tyson ecosystem wages; meth-dominant population requires specialized programming
Rank #7 · North Carolina
Raleigh-Durham, NC
79
The Research Triangle is the second-largest NC metro and growing fast. Post-2023 Medicaid expansion demand is concentrated in Durham and South Raleigh — high-poverty areas adjacent to the tech/pharma employment core. SUD residential bed supply is limited relative to the newly eligible population. Duke, UNC, and WakeMed create world-class referral infrastructure. The tech employer base (Research Triangle Park, RTP) offers the best commercial payer diversification opportunity in the Southeast outside Nashville.
Ideal Facility Profile
70–90 beds · ASAM 3.5 + IOP/PHP · Target Durham or south Raleigh (high Medicaid density near tech corridor)
Key Referral Sources
Duke University Hospital, UNC Hospitals, WakeMed, Durham Drug Treatment Court, NC DPS reentry
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$5.5–7.0M/yr at 87% · 65% Medicaid · 35% commercial
Key Risks
Real estate among NC's highest; competitive for clinical staff (competing with Duke/UNC Health salaries); more managed care complexity than western NC
Rank #8 · Tennessee
Nashville, TN
78
Nashville is the strategic pivot point for commercial payer diversification. TennCare (Medicaid) is not a full-expansion program, which limits Medicaid volume — but Nashville's massive corporate employer base (HCA Healthcare HQ, Vanderbilt, Amazon, Oracle) creates genuine commercial insurance volume. OD death rate is high (38/100K). No CON law. The behavioral health industry itself is concentrated in Nashville, meaning referral networks and clinical talent are both accessible. Best commercial payer story in the Southeast for a SUD platform.
Ideal Facility Profile
60–80 beds · ASAM 3.5 + detox · Lean commercial (target 50%+ commercial) · East Nashville or Antioch submarket
Key Referral Sources
Vanderbilt University Medical Center, HCA TriStar hospitals, Nashville Drug Court, corporate EAP programs, Music Row entertainment industry
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$5.0–6.5M/yr at 85% · 50% Medicaid / 50% commercial (best commercial mix available)
Key Risks
TennCare limitations reduce Medicaid pool; higher operating costs than other TN cities; very competitive private-pay rehab market already established
Rank #9 · South Carolina
Greenville, SC
77
Greenville is SC's fastest-growing city, with BMW, Michelin, and GE Power driving a significant working-class manufacturing base. OD death rate tracks SC's statewide average (37/100K — among the Southeast's highest). Treatment infrastructure is underdeveloped relative to need. IMD waiver is active. The primary challenge is SC's non-expansion of Medicaid — but the manufacturing employer base creates a commercial insurance offset that few other SC markets offer. CON law is the structural hurdle; must acquire an existing licensed facility.
Ideal Facility Profile
50–70 beds · ASAM 3.5 + detox · Target manufacturing workforce · Medicaid + commercial · Must acquire existing CON
Key Referral Sources
Prisma Health Upstate, Bon Secours St. Francis, Greenville County Drug Court, BMW/Michelin EAP programs, Salvation Army referrals
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$3.8–4.8M/yr at 85% · 55% Medicaid · 45% commercial (manufacturing base)
Key Risks
SC Medicaid non-expansion limits Medicaid pool significantly vs. NC/AR/WV; CON acquisition required adds 6–18 months; lower Medicaid rates (~$230/day vs $335 in LA)
Rank #10 · Tennessee
Chattanooga, TN
76
Chattanooga sits at the TN/GA border and serves as the SUD treatment hub for the entire southeastern Tennessee / northwestern Georgia corridor. OD death rate is 41/100K — above the TN average. Competition is notably lighter than Nashville or Knoxville. No CON law enables fast market entry. Volkswagen, Amazon, and TVA employment create a moderate commercial insurance pool. The city's position between two major metro markets (Nashville 2h, Atlanta 2h) makes it an underrated acquisition target with less competitive pressure than either anchor city.
Ideal Facility Profile
50–70 beds · ASAM 3.5 + 3.7 detox · Serve TN/GA border catchment · Hamilton County + Catoosa County referral draw
Key Referral Sources
CHI Memorial Hospital, Erlanger Health System, Hamilton County Drug Court, Chattanooga Area jail diversion, TN DCS
Est. Stabilized Revenue
$3.5–4.5M/yr at 85% · 68% Medicaid · 32% commercial
Key Risks
TennCare limitations (same as Nashville); border market complexity (GA patients may have different MCO credentialing requirements); smaller talent pool than major metros
